DELHI

Overview

From historical monuments to crowded shopping malls, from Mughal gardens to Delhi University campus, the capital city has multiple personalities making it 'The good, the bad and the ugly'. The Capital city of India and the base location to explore North India, Delhi is considered to be the city with a heart. Delhi has a plethora of things for tourists to do, and it is up to you to pick and choose which items you want to tick off your list, depending on what you want to see and where you want to go. If Delhi’s rich Mughal history pulls you, then you should take time to explore historical monuments such as the Red Fort, Jama Masjid, Humayun’s Tomb and Purana Qila. Delhi has famous temples scattered all across the city, a few noteworthy ones being the Akshardham Temple, the Lotus Temple (also known as the Bahai Temple), and the ISKON Temple

Delhi is a melting pot of diverse races and cultures, and this fact is amply reflected in the culinary choices of the city, which keep changing as you keep walking down different roads. Delhi is a shopper’s paradise, and the vast variety of markets in Delhi ensure that travellers go back home with double the luggage they came with!


POPULAR PLACES

RED FORT

The 33-metre long line-up of the red sandstone walls of the Red Fort is a reminder of the great power that Mughals exerted over Delhi. Even now, the buildings inside the fort can make your senses reel under the cumulative impact of marble, gilded pillars, delicate carvings, and the realization that it was inlaid with precious gems at one point in time. Commissioned by Shah Jahan in 1638, this attraction took over 10 years to complete. A UNESCO World Heritage Site, it sprawls over approximately 254 acres and has beautiful carvings on its walls. The Diwan-I-Am with its painted canopy and the Khas Mahal with its intricate marble jaali are the highlights of this place.

INDIA GATE

Straight down the road from the Rashtrapati Bhavan gate, the road called Rajpath ends in the All-India War Memorial Arch, now called India Gate. It stands 40-feet high to commemorate the Indian soldiers killed in World War I. The vast green lawns surrounding the arch are popular with the crowd. Delhites descend to this attraction on summer nights for enjoying a scoop of ice-cream. You see a lot of people having picnics at this spot during winter afternoons. Kids like to run freely on the large lawns surrounding the arch. At night, the memorial gets beautifully lit up and is a sight to behold. It has also been witness to many candle light marches and processions.

QUTUB COMPLEX

Qutub Minar is the star attraction of Qutb complex. It is a 73-metre high tower, the construction of which was started by Qutubuddin Aibak but finished by his son-in-law Iltutmish. It was built to celebrate Muslim dominance over Delhi after the defeat of the last Hindu ruler. It is five storey tower that becomes narrower as it goes up thereby providing an illusion of extra height. The two topmost minars, which stand out for being made of marble instead of red sandstone, were added later by Firoz Shah Tughlaq. The highest tower in India, Qutub Minar shows different architectural styles from Aibak to Tughlaq era.

HUMAYUN'S TOMB

One of the first fine examples of Mughal architecture in India, Humayun's Tomb is a magnificent garden tomb located in the eastern part of Delhi. A UNESCO World Heritage Site, its onion-shaped dome is a forerunner of the classic Mughal dome which became more shapely with time. The garden squares with its water channels, pathways and gardens are beautifully restored. Humayun's mausoleum is situated in the center topped by a Persian-style double-dome marble structure. The complex also incorporates the tomb of Isa Khan, the Nai ka Gumbad and Nili Gumbad. You can see intricately carved arches around the whole perimeter of the building.

LOTUS TEMPLE

Lotus Temple, also known as the Bahai House of Worship, is a wonderful place to enjoy silence in the noisy city of Delhi. Styled after a lotus flower, it serves as the mother temple of the Indian subcontinent. Designed by Iranian-Canadian architect Fariburz Sahba, the temple was built to bring faiths together. It has 27 immaculate marble petals of a lotus. The particular flower was chosen as its an important symbol in Hinduism, Islam, Buddhism and Jainism. Around the petals are nine pools of water that look spectacular in the evening when light shadows fall on its surface. People of any religion are welcome to pray and meditate silently in the central hall of this place.

AKSHARDHAM TEMPLE

The magnanimous structure and ethereal beauty of Akshardham dominates the eastern suburbs of Delhi. Also known as Swaminarayan Akshardham, the elements of this temple draw inspiration from traditional Orissan, Gujarati, Mughal and Rajasthani architecture. Rajasthani pink sandstone and Italian Carrara marble are the only materials used to construct the temple. Enter this magnificent place and the interiors will blow your mind. There are more than 20,000 carved deities, saints and mythical beings. A 3.4-metre high statue of Swaminarayan is the presiding deity here. The center of the compound having 43-metre high monument is the highlight of this place

AGRASEN KI BAOLI

Designated as a protected monument by Archaeological Survey of India, Agra Sain Ki Baoli is also known as Ugrasen ki Baoli. It is a historical step well 60-metres long and 15-metres wide, situated on Hailey Road near Connaught Place in New Delhi. Though there is no proof of the builder of this attraction, it is said that legendary Maharaj Agrasen of Mahabharat epic era built it and then it was rebuilt by Agrawal community in the 14th century. Baoli is a Hindi word for step well. It is also referred to as bauri or baori in different dialects. This place is a major tourist destination and has 103 steps consisting of 3 levels. Each level is lined with arched niches on both sides.

Languages Spoken

The most popular language in Delhi is Hindi, spoken by 81% of the population. English is also widely spoken and is used for official purposes. 7% of the population speaks Punjabi and 6% of the people speak Urdu.

Best Season to Visit

The best time to visit Delhi is from October to March. The months of October and November are pleasant, with an average temperature between 15°C and 25°C.

Must try

1.Browse Asia's Largest Wholesale Spice Market. Khari Baoli Road, next to Fatehpuri Masjid at the western end of Chandni Chowk in Old Delhi, is home to the largest wholesale spice market in Asia.
2.Listen to Qawwalis at Nizamuddin Dargah. On Thursday evenings, its courtyard erupts with the soulful sound of live qawwalis (Sufi devotional songs) accompanied by traditional Indian instruments, which serenade the audience into a trance.
3.Attend the Changing of Guard at Rashtrapati Bhavan.
4.Learn about the underbelly of Delhi on a guided walk through streets of Paharganj and the area around New Delhi Railway Station.

Shopping

The main street of Old Delhi, Chandni Chowk is the heart of Delhi. Chaotically competing for space, its narrow lanes are filled with shops to buy inexpensive stuff. Also, Street food just doesn’t get better than Chandni Chowk. Also, An underground market located between the inner and outer circle of Connaught Place, Palika Bazar houses 15,000 people within its confines at any given time. Set up in the late 1970s, it has 380 numbered shops selling a varied range of objects where electronic items and clothing are dominant and is one of the best places to visit in Delhi for all the shopaholics.

How to reach


BY FLIGHT

Indira Gandhi International Airport serves as Delhi’s domestic and international airport. The airport is well-connected to most of the cities in India and abroad. From Mumbai to Moscow or from Bangalore to Beijing, one can easily find flights for Delhi from any corner of the world.

BY RAIL

Delhi serves as the headquarter of Northern Railway. There are five main stations in Delhi, namely Delhi Junction Railway Station, New Delhi Railway Station, Nizamuddin Railway Station, Anand Vihar Railway Terminal and Delhi Sarai Rohilla Railway Station. These stations have connectivity to most parts of the country and serve millions of passengers every day. Rajdhani Express and Shatabdi Express are some of the superfast trains operating in Delhi.

BY ROAD

Various national highways, namely NH1, NH2, NH8, NH 10 and NH24, pass through Delhi. A number of public and private transport services provide A/C and non-A/C buses daily, through numerous bus terminals, scattered throughout the city. Inter-state buses from most cities and towns in the neighbouring states, pass through Delhi. Inter State Bus Terminus (ISBT) at Kashmiri Gate, Anand Vihar Bus Terminus and Sarai Kale-Khan Bus Terminus, serve as major bus terminals in the city. Some of the private bus services in Delhi are City Land Travels, Laxmi Holidays, Ashok Travels, Hari Travels, etc. Delhi Transport Corporation also operates on many inter-state routes.
